Mid Morning

●Sarah dropped me off at the bar about 9:30. My mind filled with thoughts of computers. She wanted to buy three of them. One for her home office and two for the Iron Horse. She wants the new IBM ATs, 286 processors, 512K of RAM—amazing.

●I was surprised to see all the activity at the bar—so many beautiful bikes—the really old ones showing their age earned my smile.

●This was the Club's church day. All the members in a meeting at the club room.

●I had to clear out by 10. Not allowed on Club property during church—so strange. Not a problem, either. I'd been itching for the time and excuse to walk the BN right of way east to see the trestle over the Flathead River.

●I ducked into my room and changed out of yesterday's dealer shirt & jeans into proper hiking gear, shorts and headband. The earring got left on the table.

●I crossed the tracks at the end of the building and turned east. From this perspective, I got the idea that this wasn't the railroad's original alignment—easy to see the remains of branching patterns in the layout of the land, not parallel with the current single track.

●I glanced over to the Iron Horse and it hit me. Those traces line up with the four huge bay door of the building. It wasn't a truck shop, it was a railcar maintenance building. It made perfect sense—the connection made me grin.

●The tracks curved to the right just before crossing the Flathead and a control semaphore stood sentinel before the curve, a red eye staring down toward me—westbound train coming. I picked up the pace.

●The trestle followed the pattern I hoped for, a steep gravel bank to the river, but with a level space between the abutment and the drop off. Just enough space to standup right under the tracks.

●Never really trusted the safety of it. I couldn't stand still, paced around looking alternately between the ground and the structure of the acrid creosoted beams and ties above my head.

●The rumble started. My gaze leveled with the underside the steel trusses reaching out over the river to the first concrete pier with its foot in the river—probably dated to the twenties or thirties.

●By the creeks and groans of the metal and the metallic shrieks from the rails, I felt the train getting closer and closer. The trusses flexed under the weight—subtle, but real. I figured it happened—first time I'd seen it in real life.

●The rumble vibrated my chest, my ribs tuning forks in sympathetic vibration. The first SD40 drum rolled over me, the second, a syncopation in the vibration, the third brought a symphonic climax. Diesel exhaust, hot metal, dust, grease. Goddamn, someday, I'm gonna time a jerk off a load with this—hell, imagine fucking to this rhythm.

●Without the engines, the rest of the train decrescendoed to just the metal screams and the occasional bad axle bearings banging like clockwork blacksmith hammers.

●After the last car squealed by, I scoped out a route down to the river, giddy with the thrill. Expected I'd carry that with me for the next hour.

●Wasn't much worse than hiking down a talus slope. The river cut a bench of glacial outwash, cross bedded, largely unsorted. The banks shrank as the river turned toward the US2 bridge and on to the lake.

●Most of these lower banks wore riparian cottonwoods, short lived trees that leave tangles of logs. I made my way downstream when I heard another train. Turned in time to see the eastbound Amtrak Empire Builder, a silver and blue streak across the trestle. I calculated the time and distance—seemed about five hours late, accounting for distance, won't pass Railroad Creek Ranch until late afternoon—usually noon. A treat for the passengers, it usually skirted Glacier Park at night.

●I ate my nuts and dried fruit from my pack for lunch.

●I hiked all the way to US2 and climbed up onto the highway. I passed the Glacier View Motel and then on to the Iron Horse a half mile further down the highway.
