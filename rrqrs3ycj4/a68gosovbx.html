Sarah's Office

●———> rewrite this entire section as a conversation between Karl and Sarah, move it all the way back to late August

●"Sarah, the accounting of the bar and the club doesn't make sense. I think I know why."

●Sarah looked at me with a scowl.

●"That bar couldn't make enough money to support that house you guys have. I can't figure where the money's coming from. I know Dolph's guys do security jobs. Still not enough."

●"We got our ways. The Horse makes a lot more than you'd think. 'specially the poker tables."

●"Still doesn't work out."

●"Bar ain’t the only business we got. Chris 'n I run another operation that brings in most of the money."

●I cocked my head at her—slight "go on" nod.

●"VHS tapes. Chris sells thousands and thousands of them."

●"He bootlegs movies?"

●"No. He’s got his own studio. Shoots his own stuff."

●I thought for a moment. "Porn?" I nodded as that could be the only answer.

●"Yeah. Big money in tapes. We run it together."

●"Studio isn't here is it? You and Chris spend the winters in Reno. That why."

●She nodded back.

●"Ok, that fills a gap." I looked down to the floor thinking.

●"It's not the only thing we run. We got the casino and hospitality businesses, too".

●I looked up. "Here's what I want to know." Switched to my steely expression, one eyebrow down hard. "Any drug connections?"

●She laughed. "No, Vince wouldn't go for that. Too hot."

●"So I gotta know, what are the parallel books for?"

●She hesitated, her face going flat. "Parallel books?"

●"Sarah, I taught to you how to create the books for your businesses in Peachtree. I don't know anything about accounting, but there are two businesses on the computer, Sarah1 and Sarah2. It's none of my business to know what they're for. I looked at 'em. They're not just you randomly testing things, too much detail to not be real. I need to teach you how to hide them better. Anyone with access to this computer could find them."

●She kept her deadpan face.

●I shook my head. "Ok, I'll back off. Just know, if they are what I think they are, you need to keep them off the computer's hard drive. Put them on floppies so you can secure them."

●Her heavy sigh surprised me.

●"I'm trying to not have to rely on the safe."

●"I don't know any tools on PCs that will encrpyt your files, but its possible."

●"Encrypt?"

●"Turn it into a secret code that only you can unlock."

●"You can do that?"

●I thought for a few moments, "...yes. There's a couple ways it could be done. I'd have to study it." I thought for a bit, again. "There's two parts, hiding and obfuscating."

●Her silence prompted me to go on. "Hiding is like putting it in your safe, only you know the combination. Obfuscating is making sure only you can read it once its out of the safe. It's a cypher, a secret code."

●I frowned. "You'd have to decypher it so that Peachtree could read it." I looked up to the ceiling as I paused. "Yeah, then you'd have to re cypher it. It'd look suspicious on a floppy if somebody were to audit the computer."

1984-08-14 - The Iron Horse

"I can do it." I dropped a paper diagram on Sarah's desk."

She looked up at me in confusion. "...uh, what's this?"

"It's a design to make the data on your PeachTree data disk only readable by you. You use another floppy disk as a key first. If you don't use the key disk, the computer'll think its a bad disk. It can't read it. When you do use the key, you can read the data fine until you reboot or turn the computer off."

"You can make do that?" She looked skeptical.

I dropped the sheaf of paper on top of the design, forty pages of hand written C and Assembly code in the tiniest print I could write. "I think it'll work. I just need to try it. I need a compiler, an editor, and the time to type this in. to work on it."

"How does it work?"

I smiled, excited to reveal the secret. "The key is hooking the INT13 interrupt. I can make the floppy drive do whatever I want. It it's a TSR, Terminate and Stay Resident. Peachtree wont't even know its writing to the disk in a crazy way."

She started paging through the sheets of paper. Looking closely at the code on several pages, before looking back at me. "I don't get it. What is this?"

"It's all the C code to make an executable file. I've studied how to write software for MS-DOS. I know I can make it work. I just need to get my hands on a computer."

"I don't know what any of this means." She looked both confused and concerned. "How much will it cost to get what you need?"

"I want the Lattice C compiler."
