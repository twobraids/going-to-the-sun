Bison Lakes Campsite

●I felt it, but I didn't understand it. Not steady enough to be a locomotive laboring up the pass a few miles north. Too close—slightly the wrong direction. The vibration—felt it more than heard it spun up and down as if laboring—heavy equipment.

●Scanned around my campsite assessing my readiness to head out south to Two Medicine Ridge. My two day cache hung from a tree about forty yards from my tent site. I left it there the weekend the Horse Burned.

●The day Vince died. The day the Bastards died.

●I had another cache a day east up the ridge to North Half Dome Craigs.

●My adventure laid out before, ready to go—before everything changed—again.

●I hefted my pack on my back, wiggled it into position, then drew the straps tighter. The load shifted from my shoulders to resting by friction on my hips.

●Once I got out from the glacial bowl holding my beloved Bison Lakes, the vibration became clear. The blight yellow bulldozers chewed at the hillside above the river to my north. The new road into a territory that never had such a magnitude of insult since era of the glaciers.

●My trail, descending into the river valley, started as a deer trail. Turned into just hoofing it across open hillsides. Dry alpine country. The summer wildflowers in monochrome drifts before me. Yellow, the main color of July, Arrow Leaf Balsom Root. Looked like good terrain for Bitterroot, but I didn't see any, a bit late, even here.

●As the pitch of my descent increased, my walk cadence shifted to my heels—traction, stability. Not a rocky canyon, this river cut through old glacial till, gravel, boulders

●I grinned hard as I got to the river. It's not big, but big enough to earn the name river. The coolest thing, only riverbanks parallel it. No roads. No pipelines. No powerlines. This was wilderness—not with a capital W—virgin land sacred

●I stood there listening. Couldn't hear the bulldozers. They cheated. They didn't push through the canyon, they cut from US2 on Marias Pass, over that gentler terrain to intercept the river higher upslope from where I stood.

●They wanted to rape this virgin land. Leave her with a bloody scar across her face visible from the Reservation. Show them who's in charge.

●The water had calmed a lot since last time I hiked this way. I worked my way upstream until I spotted my crossing point. A series of nice conveniently placed boulders, underwater most of the year, that I could step without much thought across. Mettler Coulee.

●Once across, though, the job became much more difficult. It faced north, that meant less sun, but more water, denser trees and undergrowth. Took me about forty minutes to get back up into the more open ridge terrain.

●Now had to follow along the bare west side of the parallel ridges, cross one perpendicular draining then hit the ridge above Hyde Creek. From there it was a straight hike up to the road—effing microwave tower on Mt Baldy—how many Mt Baldys in the world? My camp site sat nestled between boulders below the peak in a draw with a great view east to the morning sun. From the tops of the protective boulders, my final destination beckoned only a few rugged miles on the ridgeline south.

●The south side of the bare Mt Baldy blushed with pink flowers. I knew them well, bitterroots.

●The flat sandy level spot gave me just enough room for my tent. Didn't take long to set it up. My cache of firewood waited patiently under the overhang just above the tent site.

●I confess. I picked the lock on the gate for the road to Mt Baldy. I provisioned this camp and caching site with my Subaru. The locking box hidden in the rocks, so far, hadn't been tested by bears.

●As night fell, I sat on the highest boulder smoking a joint. The shadow on the front range mountains stretched out, longer and longer, into the land of the Blackfeet, until lost in the haze of the horizon.
