I woke long before dawn, the Milky Way arched from the eastern horizon—faint, swinging north then descending to the west—bright. The moon hung to my right in the south, only a third of its face lit. To the north, my great bear hung in the sky watching over me.

Warm and comfortable in my sleeping bag, I waited for the zodiacal light. Not really prominent this time of the year, I didn’t bank on seeing it. One at a time, the three stars of Orion’s Belt rose from under the horizon. First time I’d ever seen Orion in the summer.

A half hour later, I got the nut flush I hoped for: the Zodiacal Light. The faint triangle of light, just a few degrees north of due east, appeared in the sky. Not really very visible looking directly, I caught it only by side-eyeing the horizon. It's really a fall or spring phenomenon, never strong in the mid-summer.

Another half hour later, at the first stab of sunlight hit the tallest peaks Glacier Park. Every few moments another mountain would light up. They all caught it before I did, my peak stood a thousand feet shorter.

The closest park peak now lit was, of course, the wall of Calf Robe. I remembered standing on top of it, looking south to this very peak. I didn't know its name until I had gotten home and located it on the topo maps in my loft.

I nestled back down into my sleeping bag. Probably below freezing out there. Over the years of climbing to peaks, I'd noticed that dawn always kick starts the winds. I wanted a few minutes longer in the warmth I had banked through the night.

Didn't mean to, but I slipped back to sleep. From the angle of the sun, about fifteen degrees off the horizon, I figured I had slept another hour.

I pulled my backpack closer, digging into one of the pockets for my bag of granola trail mix. Once I got my water bottle opened and I downed half of it, I realized how strongly I needed to pee.

While I was naked in the sleeping bag, I learned long ago that sleeping with my clothes stashed in with me meant putting on warm clothes instead cold ones. I struggled a bit, but I got myself dressed before I climbed out the sleeping bag into the cold air.

Standing on the highest boulder of the peak and unzipping the pants that I had zipped up only seconds earlier, I arced a pee stream out over the edge. I watched it spatter onto that rocky chute that I had considered climbing yesterday. My stream undermined a fist sized rock. It tumbled, gathered a cohort of companion pebbles and sailed over the headwall to the cirque below. Had I tried to climb it, that would have been me plummeting down the cliff.

I needed to get out of the wind. With my grip firmly on the rope, lowered pack back down the cleft I had climbed yesterday. Once sitting on the ground, I let the rope drop.

There was alway a moment when leaving a mountain peak, a tiny electrostatic shock of disappointment. Most peaks are one time experiences, there were always more of them, few would ever see my return.

Bracing my legs on the far boulder, I chimneyed down between the twin rocks, reversing yesterday's moves. The pika sitting on rock near my pack had only harsh words for me regarding my trespass: a series of high pitched barks of alarm as it eyed me. I flashed an open palm to it and it vanished behind its rock. Just two seconds later, from a rock six feet above me, it restated its objections in no uncertain terms.

With the rope recoiled and lashed to the pack, I hefted it onto my shoulders and left the pika in peace.

My entire route laid in front of me. I wasn't retracing my path from yesterday. I was going straight over to the ridge and down into Hyde Creek Canyon. I would follow it all the way down to the Two Medicine River, then climb back up to check out what the fuck those bulldozers were doing.

An hour later, Hyde Creek canyon astounded me. From my vantage point on a rock outcrop, I could see about a mile and a half of its course. Rank after rank of white water rapids, still looking formidable even though we were a couple months beyond the highest of the season snowmelt. No way I could hike the canyon floor, best stay on the ridge and then drop to the confluence with the river.

I took the opportunity to sit with my backpack's weight sitting on the rock behind me. I fished in my pocket for the plastic bag and my lighter. I pulled out the second to the last joint and smiled: totally sweet spot for a mid morning toke over the canyon.

The first draw tasted foul, like I'd just lit a literal roach. I spit to try to get the taste out of my mouth. I examined the joint, it had gone out. No insect. I tapped the ash off of it and pinched the end back to unburned leaf.

The lighter made its click, spark, and tiny whoosh as I pulled in another lungful—explosive coughing. The thing was totally wrong. Coughed so hard I got dizzy.

I tore the joint open. White stuff all over the leaf. Moldy? I just sucked a moldy joint.

The bottom dropped out of my mind.
